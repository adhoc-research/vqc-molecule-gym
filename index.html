vqc-molecule-gym

An RL environment for variational quantum circuits in molecular ground-state estimation

The environment frames molecular ground-state estimation as a sequential decision task. An agent proposes a sequence of operator-pool entries with optional per-operator variational angles; the environment validates the proposal, constructs a circuit from a Hartree-Fock reference state, simulates the statevector on a CUDA-Q backend, and scores the resulting molecular energy against exact diagonalization. A shaped reward accounts for energy error, chemical accuracy, circuit depth, and shot cost. The figures below summarise evaluation logs from three baseline search algorithms across seven molecular benchmark families.

Molecular benchmark families

1 · Task formulation

Molecular ground-state estimation as a sequential decision task

Each task specifies a molecule, geometry, basis set, active space, fermion-to-qubit mapping, an operator pool, and an exact energy reference. An agent submits a structured JSON action consisting of a sequence of pool operators, optional per-operator angles (in radians), and a shot count. The environment validates and evaluates the action and returns the molecular energy, the energy error relative to exact diagonalization, a chemical-accuracy flag, circuit metrics, and a scalar reward.

4 · Results

Reward decomposition and circuit cost

The reward combines an energy-error term with additional terms for chemical accuracy, circuit depth, shot cost, and operator compactness. The first panel decomposes the reward of the highest-scoring circuit for each benchmark; the second relates circuit depth to energy error for individual circuits.

R = 0.90 · s(ε) + 0.08 · 𝟙[ε ≤ ε*] + 0.01 · sdepth + 0.005 · sshots + 0.005 · scompact
s(ε) = 1 − 2(ε / ε*) ⁄ (1 + ε / ε*)
  • ε absolute energy error in milli-Hartree, relative to exact diagonalization
  • ε* chemical-accuracy threshold, 1.6 mHa (1 kcal mol⁻¹)
  • s(ε) bounded error score: 0 mHa maps to +1, ε* maps to 0, large errors asymptote to −1
  • sdepth, sshots, scompact resource scores in [0, 1] for circuit depth, shot count, and operator count
  • 𝟙[ε ≤ ε*] chemical-accuracy bonus, 1 when the circuit clears the threshold

Conclusion

Summary of results

Across 506 evaluations over 50 molecular tasks in seven benchmark families, the three baseline agents establish a clear ordering and a calibrated difficulty range for the environment. Beam search dominates the fixed-search baselines, and the attainable error separates the benchmarks into three curriculum tiers.

  • Beam search is the strongest baseline. Under the shared evaluator contract, beam search reaches the lowest median energy error on every family and posts the highest chemical-accuracy hit rate; greedy search is competitive on the easy tier, while random sampling rarely clears the 1.6 mHa threshold.
  • The easy tier is effectively solved. H₂, LiH, and C₂H₆ reach chemical accuracy on every geometry (5/5, 8/8, and 7/7 tasks), with attainable median errors of 0.002, 1.23, and 0.23 mHa — a dense, reliable reward signal for early policy learning.
  • The medium tier improves but stalls near the threshold. H₂O and the water dimer (H₂O)₂ track toward chemical accuracy without reaching it (median errors of about 6.4 and 2.5 mHa), limited by the current operator pools and angle grids.
  • The hard tier remains open. H₄ and N₂ show large gains over the Hartree-Fock base yet stall above 10 mHa (median 22 and 84 mHa), marking them as evaluation-only tasks until the operator pools and active spaces are extended.

Overall, 142 of 506 evaluated circuits reach chemical accuracy, concentrated in the easy tier. These baselines set the reference frontier against which the learned policies in future work will be measured.

Implementation

Software and dependencies

The environment depends on CUDA-Q and CUDA-QX for circuit simulation and molecular data, with PySCF providing the local molecule backend. Actions and results are validated with Pydantic schemas, and the environment is exposed through a Verifiers adapter.

Future work

Next steps

The current results rely on random, greedy, and beam-search baselines. Planned work replaces these fixed search procedures with learned policies and connects the environment to language-model post-training.

  • Learned policies with PPO over a GNN. Train an agent with proximal policy optimization (PPO) using a graph neural network (GNN) over the molecular graph and the candidate operator pool, so that a single policy generalizes across geometries rather than searching each task independently.
  • Language-model post-training in the environment. Use the environment as a reinforcement-learning signal for post-training a Nemotron model, with the language model proposing circuits as actions and being optimized against the environment reward.
  • Larger operator pools and active spaces. Extend the operator pools and active spaces for the harder benchmarks (H4 and N2), where the attainable error currently stalls above chemical accuracy.
